1. Introduction¶

The strain energy function \(W\) is defined as:

(1)¶\[ W=W(I_1 (\textbf {C}),I_2 (\textbf {C}),I_3 (\textbf {C}))\]

In the above equations, \(\textbf {C}\) is the right Cauchy deformation tensor:

(2)¶\[\textbf {C}=F^TF\]

Where \(F\) is the deformation gradient tensor: \(F=I+\nabla u\). The Jacobian of \(F\) is defined as its determinant: \(J= \lvert F \rvert\).

The \(I_1 (\textbf {C})\), \(I_2 (\textbf {C})\) and \(I_3 (\textbf {C})\) are the invariants of the \(\textbf {C}\) tensor.

(3)¶\[ \begin{align}\begin{aligned}I_1=tr(\textbf{C})\\I_2=0.5[(tr \textbf{C})^2-tr(\textbf{C}^2)]\\I_3=det (\textbf{C})\end{aligned}\end{align} \]

The second Piola-Kirchhoff stress is defined as:

(4)¶\[S=2 \frac{\partial W}{\partial \textbf {C}}\]

After applying the chain rule, we can write the term \(\frac{\partial W}{\partial \textbf {C}}\) as:

(5)¶\[\frac{\partial W}{\partial \textbf {C}}=\frac{\partial W}{I_1} \frac{\partial I_1}{\textbf {C}} + \frac{\partial W}{I_2} \frac{\partial I_2}{\textbf {C}}+\frac{\partial W}{I_3} \frac{\partial I_3}{\textbf {C}}\]

The first term on the right hand side, we could apply the chain rul to calculate the \(\frac{\partial I_1}{\partial C}\):

(6)¶\[ \begin{align}\begin{aligned}\frac{\partial I_1}{\partial C}=\frac{\partial (I_1)_{11}}{C_{ij}} e_i \otimes e_j + \frac{\partial (I_1)_{22}}{C_{ij}} e_i \otimes e_j + \frac{\partial (I_1)_{33}}{C_{ij}} e_i \otimes e_j\\\frac{\partial I_1}{\partial C}=e_1 \otimes e_1 +e_2 \otimes e_2 +e_3 \otimes e_3 = \textbf{I}\end{aligned}\end{align} \]

Similary we can can derive the \(\frac{\partial I_2}{\partial C}\) and \(\frac{\partial I_3}{\partial C}\):

(7)¶\[ \begin{align}\begin{aligned}\frac{\partial I_2}{\partial \textbf {C}}= I_1 \textbf {I} - \textbf {C}\\\frac{\partial I_3}{\partial \textbf {C}}= I_2 \textbf {I} - I_1 \textbf {C} + \textbf {C}^2\end{aligned}\end{align} \]

By plugging back the equation.6 and equation.7 into the equation.5, we can find the second piola kirchhoff stress as:

(8)¶\[\textbf {S}= 2 \alpha_1 \textbf {I} + 2 \alpha_2 \textbf {C} + 2 \alpha_3 \textbf {C}^2\]

In Equation.8:

(9)¶\[ \begin{align}\begin{aligned}\alpha_1 = \frac{\partial W}{\partial I_1} + \frac{\partial W}{\partial I_2} I_1 + \frac{\partial W}{\partial I_3} I_2\\\alpha_2 = -\frac{\partial W}{\partial I_2} - \frac{\partial W}{\partial I_3} I_1\\\alpha_3 = \frac{\partial W}{\partial I_3}\end{aligned}\end{align} \]

The Cauchy stress is defined as:

(10)¶\[\sigma = J^{-1} F F^T \textbf {S}\]

Similar to the Equation.1 we can define:

(11)¶\[ W=W(I_1 (\textbf {b}),I_2 (\textbf {b}),I_3 (\textbf {b}))\]

Where \(\textbf {b}\) is the left Cauchy deformation tensor:

(12)¶\[\textbf {b}=FF^T\]

It should be noted that the invariants of the textbf {b} are obtained similar to the equation.3. By taking the same steps shown in the equation.6 and equation.7

In addition, we take advantage of Cayley-Hamilton theorem:

(13)¶\[\textbf{b}^3-I_1 \textbf{b}^2+I_2 \textbf{b} - I_3 \textbf{I} =0\]

So the term \(\frac{\partial I_3}{\partial \textbf {b}}\) could be rewritten again:

(14)¶\[\frac{\partial I_3}{\partial \textbf {b}}= I_3 \textbf {b}^{-1}\]

We can write the \(\frac{\partial W}{\partial \textbf {b}}\):

(15)¶\[\frac{\partial W}{\partial \textbf {b}}= (\frac{\partial W}{\partial I_1} + \frac{\partial W}{\partial I_2} I_1) \textbf {I} - \frac{\partial W}{\partial I_2}\textbf {b} +\frac{\partial W}{\partial I_3} I_3 \textbf {b}^{-1}\]

After multiplying the \(\textbf {b}\) tensor into the equation.15 and then by substitution in equation.10, the stress could be defined in the form form:

(16)¶\[ \sigma=\beta_1 \textbf {I}+\beta_2 \textbf {b}+\beta_3 \textbf {b}^2\]

In the above equation:

(17)¶\[ \begin{align}\begin{aligned}\beta_1 = 2J^{-1} (\frac{\partial W}{\partial I_3}I_3)\\\beta_2 = 2J^{-1} (\frac{\partial W}{\partial I_1} + \frac{\partial W}{\partial I_2} I_1)\\\beta_3 = 2J^{-1} (-\frac{\partial W}{\partial I_2})\end{aligned}\end{align} \]
